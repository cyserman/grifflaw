Commonwealth v. Hunte: Pennsylvania Supreme Court Strikes Down Warrantless DUI Blood Draws

By Steve Griffiths, Criminal Defense Attorney | Published: January 13, 2026

On June 17, 2025, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court delivered a unanimous and unequivocal victory for constitutional rights in DUI cases. In Commonwealth v. Hunte, the Court struck down Section 3755 of the Vehicle Code, which had allowed police to conduct warrantless blood draws on unconscious DUI suspects.

The Background: Section 3755 and "Implied Consent"

For years, Pennsylvania law under 75 Pa.C.S. § 3755 permitted law enforcement to draw blood from unconscious motorists suspected of DUI without obtaining a warrant. The statute was premised on Pennsylvania's "implied consent" law.

Key Takeaway:

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court unanimously ruled that warrantless blood draws from unconscious DUI suspects violate the Fourth Amendment. Law enforcement must now obtain a warrant in virtually all cases.

The Supreme Court's Holding

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court's answer was clear and unanimous: Section 3755 is facially unconstitutional under both the federal and Pennsylvania constitutions.

The Warrant Requirement Is Not Optional

The Court began with first principles: the Fourth Amendment requires law enforcement to obtain a warrant before conducting a search, subject only to specifically defined exceptions.

"The Fourth Amendment doesn't evaporate simply because a person cannot actively assert their rights at a given moment."

"Implied Consent" Is Not Real Consent

The Court firmly rejected the Commonwealth's argument. Real consent requires a voluntary, knowing, and intelligent waiver of constitutional rights.

For DUI Defendants: If police drew your blood without a warrant while you were unconscious, that evidence should be suppressed unless the Commonwealth can prove genuine exigent circumstances existed.

The Practical Impact

This decision has immediate and far-reaching consequences for DUI enforcement in Pennsylvania:

  • Law enforcement must obtain warrants before drawing blood from unconscious suspects
  • Case-by-case exigency analysis is required
  • Hundreds of pending cases may be affected

What This Means for You

If you're facing DUI charges involving blood evidence:

It's essential to work with an attorney who understands how decisions like Hunte affect your case. Constitutional challenges to evidence can make the difference between a conviction and a dismissal.
